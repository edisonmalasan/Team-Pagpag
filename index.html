Definition & Background

What is Pagpag?

Pagpag is a type of upcycled food commonly consumed by some urban-poor communities in the Philippines as a desperate response to hunger and food insecurity. The practice involves scavenging for leftover or discarded meat, often sourced from fast-food restaurants, garbage bins, or dumpsites, which would normally be considered waste. These scraps are collected, sorted to remove visibly spoiled parts, washed, and then re-cooked before being sold as a very cheap meal to families who cannot afford fresh food. The term “pagpag” literally means to “shake off” or “brush off” dirt, symbolizing the process of cleaning and salvaging edible portions from discarded food. For many struggling households, pagpag becomes not just a meal but a means of survival, offering a low-cost source of calories despite the significant health risks it carries.

Causes & Drivers

Why Does Pagpag Exist?

Field observations suggest pagpag is a survival response driven by extreme poverty, food price pressure, and organized waste flows that enable collection and resale. Repeated consumption without obvious acute illness reinforces acceptability in some neighborhoods (Chua et al., 2018).

Poverty & Unemployment
Income instability forces families to prioritize satiety over quality; cheap leftovers fill the gap.
Food Insecurity
Limited affordable protein options push households toward risky but available alternatives.
Waste Streams
Restaurant discards create supply for an informal trade in edible scraps.
Source: Chua et al., 2018

Health Risks

From Unsafe Food To Poor Health

Immediate Risks

Foodborne illness from bacterial contamination; toxins from spoilage; gastrointestinal distress following inadequate reheating or improper handling.

Long-term Risks

Chronic undernutrition, micronutrient deficiencies, and stunting among children linked to poor diet quality and food insecurity over time.

Household Burden

Recurrent sickness and low energy reduce work attendance and income; healthcare spending diverts limited cash from food and school needs.

Child Learning & Growth

Poor diet quality contributes to missed school days, difficulty concentrating, and impaired physical growth during early years.

“Clinical assessment of children in a pagpag-consuming household showed signs of protein-energy malnutrition (extremely underweight, hair/skin dyspigmentation, muscle wasting).” Chua et al., 2018

Solutions

What Can Help?

A mix of education, governance, and livelihood support can reduce reliance on unsafe food while improving nutrition and household stability.

📗
Nutrition Education
Community sessions on safe food handling and balanced, affordable diets.
🚯
Food Waste Enforcement
Stricter handling of edible discards and safer recovery pathways.
🧵
Livelihoods
Skills and microenterprise support to boost regular income.
🍚
Feeding Programs
Targeted school and community feeding to protect child growth.
